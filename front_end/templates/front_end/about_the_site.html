What is UKBOA?


UK Box Office Analytics (UKBOA) is an interactive data visualisation tool which tracks the performance of films, actors, directors and screenwriters at the UK box office since 2001. Using the BFI's weekly box office reports, UKBOA gauges how films and the people involved in making them have seen their financial returns fluctuate over time, providing an insight into the changing tastes of British cinema-goers.

With a database of over 8000 films, 60,000 actors, 5000 directors and 13,000 screenwriters, UKBOA offers several different metrics and visually-rendered infographics to analyse and compare films and film-making talent.

UKBOA is a work in progress conceived, created and built by Ed Ludlow, a media professional currently working for the London-based film and entertainment PR firm Organic Publicity.

Ed Ludlow, Founder


"UKBOA was a lockdown project which got out of hand.

I've always been fascinated by the way market trends affect the film industry but frustated by the shortage of resources (accessible to the public) which provide information on it.

So during the four months of standstill in 2020, I decided that I would build a website that could address this. The only problem was that I'd never typed a line of code before in my life..."

How was it made?


"UKBOA draws its inspiration from Andy Rae's Box Office Dashboard which uses the BFI's weekly box office reports to track the general performance of British cinematic revenue since 2001 as well as the gross earnings of individual titles and distributors in that period.

UKBOA uses a similar data pipline, extracting the relevant information from the BFI weekly reports, transforming and cleaning it using Python Pandas before loading it into a large back-end database. However, UKBOA also integrates information and images from IMDb's OMDb API to provide its own distinct metrics and graphical interface, as well as insights into the talent involved in making the films.

UKBOA uses Django for its front-end interface, with graphs rendered through Python Plotly (using the method helpfully outlined by Richard Barella on his website: Coding w/ Ricky)"
